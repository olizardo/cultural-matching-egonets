Cultural Matching Analysis

Author

Omar Lizardo et al.

Published

June 28, 2026

Overview

This analysis file examines the role of cultural matching and network opacity in predicting the persistence of social ties over time. We pool data across adjacent waves from the NetSense study to construct a person-period dataset, restricting the sample to non-kin, on-campus student ties.

We estimate the probability of tie persistence as a function of cultural matching (closed-form domains and open-ended activities) and network opacity (the number of “Don’t Know” responses regarding an alter’s preferences) using mixed-effects logistic regression (lme4::glmer).

Baseline Models

The following models assess the main effects of closed-form and open-ended cultural matching on the odds of tie persistence. Both closed and open-ended measures independently predict persistence.

Network Opacity Models

These models incorporate network opacity. The main effects model reveals that a lack of shared cultural knowledge signals a weaker tie. Interaction models show that the protective effect of known cultural matches remains relatively stable regardless of how much else the ego doesn’t know about the alter. Following Mize (2019), non-significant interaction coefficients are supported by formal tests of second differences in average marginal effects, which are also not statistically significant.

Figure Figure 1 visualizes the marginal effect (calculated as the change from the minimum to the maximum value) of closed-form matches, open-ended matches, and network opacity on the predicted probability of tie persistence.

Model Fit Comparison

A comparative summary of the fit statistics for all models, demonstrating that the Opacity Main Effects model provides the best overall fit (lowest AIC/BIC) and that adding interaction terms does not statistically improve model fit.
